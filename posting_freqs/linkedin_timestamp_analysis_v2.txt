================================================================
  SECTION 4 — KEY FINDINGS & CONTEXT
================================================================

1. THE '4x/WEEK' WISDOM IS CLEARLY OUTDATED for high-growth creators.
   Every profile here except Jeff Putz exceeds that threshold significantly.
   The median posting rate across all 16 profiles is ~3–5 posts per week,
   with 6 profiles posting effectively every single day.

2. SCHEDULER TOOLS ARE NEAR-UNIVERSAL among high-frequency posters.
   At least 10 of 16 profiles show clear evidence of scheduled automation.
   Common tools: Buffer, Taplio, AuthoredUp, Hootsuite, or custom scripts.
   Tell-tale signs: exact recurring UTC times, posts seconds apart (carousels),
   uninterrupted daily streaks regardless of weekends or holidays.

3. CAROUSEL / MULTI-IMAGE POSTS log as MULTIPLE activity items.
   Several profiles (Christina Wodtke, Tyler Denk) have bursts of posts
   firing within 1–5 seconds of each other — these are the same post,
   just LinkedIn counting each image/slide as a separate activity event.
   This inflates 'posts per day' counts for those profiles.

4. ADAM KARPIAK IS AN EXTREME OUTLIER.
   187 posts loaded covers only 19 days (Feb 6–25, 2026).
   Average: ~9.8 posts/day. Estimated 6-month true volume: ~1,750 posts.
   This is almost certainly automated content or a very active team account.

5. JOSH BOB IS PERFECTLY METRONOMIC.
   Posts at 13:24–13:25 UTC every single day with near-zero variance.
   A human being cannot maintain this precision — this is a scheduler.

6. ETHAN MOLLICK IS THE MOST AUTHENTICALLY ORGANIC.
   Posts at all hours including 01:00–04:00 UTC (late night US Eastern).
   High frequency (4+ per week) but no discernible time pattern.
   Consistent with someone who posts when inspired rather than on schedule.

post creation time via snowflake-style IDs: timestamp = (id >> 22) + 1800615
This yields exact UTC millisecond precision. LinkedIn's feed cap (~185–200
visible posts) means high-volume posters like Adam Karpiak are significantly
undercounted in the 6-month totals from the previous relative-timestamp analysis.
